1. The sorted list predicate defined mathematically

The mathematical definition of whether a list is sorted is defined as follows:

1.1. Definition (sorted list)

Defn: A list [x0 … xn] is sorted iff for each 0<=i<=j<=n, i<=j IMPLIES xi <= xj.

2. Sorted as a judgement

2.1. Definition (Judgements about sorted lists)

Defn: A judgement J about sorted lists is written as sorted(p), where p is a list.

A judgement about a list may or may not be "true". E.g, here are examples of judgements, only some of which are true:

  • sorted([])
  • sorted([2 8 3])
  • sorted([3 1])

2.2. Discussion

It is easy to write an algorithm that determines whether a list is sorted or not. Programming Language theorists, however, find it useful to employ Systems of Logic, i.e., a set of rules (along with some axioms) that are used to derive judgements.

3. A deductive system for well formed judgements about sorted lists

Here is a deductive system D for sorted lists. The motivation is that D will somehow correctly capture the notion of sorted lists. (This notion will be made precise soon.) 


----------   N
sorted []


 -----------   S
 sorted [x] 


 sorted [y . p]    x <=y
 ------------------------  L
     sorted [x y . p]

In the above rules statement(s) above the line -— is the antecedent and the statement below the line is the consequent. If the antecedent is true, then using this deduction system it can be said that consequent is true.

Let J is a judgement, then D(J) asserts that J is derivable using the rules of D.

3.1. Exercises

Derive the following judgements using the system of rules D:

  1. sorted []
  2. sorted [2 5]
  3. sorted [2 8 9]

3.2. Soundness and completeness of D wrt the definition of a sorted list

What use is this logical system D? This question may be broken into two separate questions that together help answer whether the logical system D adequately captures the notion of sortedness of a list.

  1. Theorem (Soundness of D): If sorted(p) is derivable from

the system D of logic given above, then p is indeed sorted.

  1. Theorem (Completeness of D): If p is a sorted list, then

there is a derivation (proof) of sorted(p) using the rules of the logic D.

The Soundness and Completeness theorems of D are examples of meta-logical reasoning, i.e., which is mathematical reasoning of a formal logical theory, in this case the theory generated by the deduction system D.

3.2.1. Theorem-sorted-soundness

Proof is by induction on the derivation of D and is left as an exercise

3.2.2. Theorem-sorted-completeness

Proof is by induction on the inductive construction of p as a list and is left as an exercise.

3.3. Additional Lemmas

The following Lemmata are additional examples of meta-logical reasoning:

3.3.1. 1. Lemma (sorted implies tail is sorted)

If D(sorted([y . p])), then D(sorted(p).

Proof: Left as an exercise.

3.3.2. 2. Lemma (sorted implies head element is minimal)

If D(sorted([y . p])), then y is less than equal to every element of p.

Proof: by induction on the derivation of sorted([y . p]).

Base case S: vacuously true.

Induction case: Assume the

Inductive hypothesis: for all derivations, D(sorted([x . q])), x is less than equal to every element of q.

Show that if

D(sorted([y x . q])) then y is less than equal to every element of [x . q]

Assume D(sorted([y x . q])).

Then, D must be of the form 


sorted([x . q]), y <= x
-------------------------
sorted([y x . q])

where D([x . q]).

Let a belongs to q. Since D([x . q]), x <= a by the Induction Hypothesis and instantiation Now, y <=x. Therefore y <= a. by transitivity of <=.

Thus y is less than each element of [x . q]. QED.

3.3.3. Lemma 3: (sorted implies every subsequence is sorted)

If D(sorted(p)), then for every subsequence q of p, D(sorted(q)).

Proof: by induction on p.

Base case: D(sorted([y])). Then D(sorted([])) is true using the N rule. Inductive case: Suppose D(sorted([y . p])).

If r is a any subsequence of [y . p] then one of the following true:

a. r is a subsequence of p. b. r is a [y . s] where s is a proper subsequence of p.

Then, for a: D(sorted(r)), by IH. For b:, we have D(sorted(s)). There are 3 cases:

case 1. s is empty: then D(sorted[]), and therefore D([y]) by Rule S case 2. s is nonempty: then s = [z . t]. By Lemma 2, y is less than every element of s. In particular y <= z. Now, using Rule L. we have 


y<= z   sorted([z . s])
--------------------------
sorted([y z . s])

4. Multiset

A multiset is a collection which admits multiple occurrences of elements. Formally a carrier set A, a multiset over A a mapping from elements of A to natural numbers.

Examples: Let A = {a, b, c, d}

Then p = {a:2, b:1, c:0, d:0} is a multiset over A.

p may also be written as {a,a,b}.

4.1. Notation

We write p(x) to denote the frequency of occurrence of x in the multiset p. Ie., p is considered as a function from the carrier A to natural numbers. In the above example where p={a, a, b}, p[a]=2, p[b]=1, and p[x] = 0 for x in {c,d}.

4.2. Multiset underlying a list

Every list p over a carrier A induces a multiset which is obtained by noting the frequency of occurrences of each element of p. Note that different lists may induce the same multiset.

4.3. Multisets differing by one element

4.3.1. Defn:

q=p+1 mod x means that

q(y) = p(y)+1 if y = x = p(y).

Example:

[2 3 8 2] = [2 8 3] + 1 mod 2

5. Sorting transformation

Given a list p, a list q is a sorting of p if

q is a permutation of p
ie., the underlying multisets of q and p are identical.
q is sorted

6. Insertion Operation

6.1. The inductive definition of insert:


---------------- NULL
insert [] x = [x]

x<=y
-----------------------------  L
insert [y . p] x =  [x y . p]

y<x,  insert p x =  q
-----------------------------  R
insert [y . p] x = [y . q]

6.2. Example

6.2.1. insert [2,5] 1 = [1,2,5]


1. 2 <=3
2. insert [3] 2 =  [2 3]    from  1 using L

6.2.2. insert [7 2 9 11 3] 8 = [7 2 8 9 11 3]


1. 8<=9
2. insert [9 11 3] 8 =  [8 9 11 3]  from 1 using L
3. 2 < 8
4. insert[2 9 11 3] 8 =  [2 8 9 11 3] from 3, 2 using R
5. 7 < 8
6. insert [7 2 9 11 3]  8 = [7 2 8 9 11 3] from 5, 4 using R

6.2.3. Finger Exercise

Informally guess what insert "does" to a list?

6.2.4. Note

Our intent in defining insert is to use it to do insertion sort. But note that insert makes no assumption that its first argument is sorted.

6.3. Lemma-insert-multiset

If D(insert p x = q), then q=p+1 mod x.

Proof: By induction on deductions of insert.

6.3.0.1. Base case NULL:

p = [], therefore p(x)=0. Clearly q(x)=1.

6.3.0.2. Base case L:

x<=y
----------------------------- L
insert [y . p]  x = [x y . p]

Let q = [y . p] and r = [x y . p].

There are 2 cases:

1. x=y

Let z be in the multiset [y . p]: There are two cases:

1. z=y
then [y . p](y) = 1 + p(y). [x y . p](y) = 2 + p(y)
2. z!=y
[y . p](z) = p(z). [x y . p](z) = p(z)

Thus [y . p] = [x y . p] mod x

2. x!=y

Again let z be an arbitrary element in [y . p]. There are 3 cases:

1. z != x AND z !=y
then [y . p](z) = p(z), whereas [x y . p](z) = p(z).
2. z = x AND z !=y
then [y . p](z) = p(z), and [x y . p](z) = 1+p(z).
3. z !=x AND z = y
then [y . p](z) = 1+p(y) and [x y . p](z) = 1+p(y)

Thus [y . p] = [x y . p] mod x

Clearly, q(y) = p(y)+1 and r(x) = q(x)+1.

6.3.0.3. Inductive case R

y<x,  insert p  x =  q
-----------------------------  R
insert [y . p] x =  [y . q]

Let z be an arbitrary element of the carrier. There are 3 cases:

6.3.0.3.1. z!=x AND z!=y
1
[y . p](z) = p(z).
2
[y . q](z) = q(z).
3
q = p+1 mod x by IH
4
q(z) = p(z) by defn of q = p+1 mod x
5
[y . p](z) = [y . q](z)
6.3.0.3.2. z=y

In this case

5
[y . p](z) = 1+p(y)
6
[y . q](y) = 1+q(y)
7
p(y) = q(y) by IH since y!=x
8
[y . p](z) = [y . q](z)
6.3.0.3.3. z=x
9
[y . p](z) = p(x)
10
[y . q](z) = q(x)
11
q(x) = 1 + p(x) BY IH
12
[y . q](x) = 1 + [y . p](x) from 9, 10, 11

From 5, 8, and 12, we may conclude that

[y . q] = [y . p] mod x

completing the inductive case when the last step of the derivation is an R.

6.3.0.4. Conclusion

From the 2 base cases and the inductive case R, we may conclude the statement if D(insert(p,x,q)) then q = p+1 mod x

6.4. Lemma-insert-sorted

This Lemma is the important one that justifies inserting as a way of sorting:

Lemma: If D(insert p x = q) and D(sorted(p)), then D(sorted(q)).

Proof by induction on derivation of insert(p,x,q)

6.4.0.1. Base case N:

Trivial.

6.4.0.2. Base case L:

x<=y
-----------------------------  L
insert [y . p]  x =  [x y . p]

7. Insertion Sort

We now proceed to define isort, or insertion sort as a logical system:

7.1. Defn of isort


-------------  N
isort [] =  []


isort p = q,  insert q  x = r
----------------------------------  L
isort [x . p] = r

7.2. Theorem-isort-soundness

If D(isort p = q), then q is a sorting of p.

Proof by induction on the derivation of isort.

7.2.1. Base case

Trivial

7.2.2. Inductive case:

Given

  1. D(isort p = q)
  2. D(insert q x = r)
  3. q is a sorting of p. (by IH)
  4. r=q+1 mod x by Lemma-insert-multiset

We need to prove that

  1. r is a sorting of [x . p], ie.,
  2. r is a permutation of [x . p]
  3. r is sorted.

Now,

  1. sorted(q) from 3 using Theorem-sorted-soundness
  2. sorted(r) from 8 using Lemma-insert-sort

This proves 7.

To prove 6, we show that

  1. r(z) = [x . p](z) for any z in the carrier:

There are 2 cases:

7.2.2.1. z = x :
  1. r(x) = q(x)+1 from 4. and defn of mod
  2. q(x) = p(x) from 3 (q is a sorting of p)
  3. [x . p](x) = p(x)+1 from defn of multiset.
  4. r(x) = [x . p] p(x)+1 from 11, 12, and 13 using =
7.2.2.2. z !=x :
  1. r(z) = q(z) from 4 and defn of mod
  2. q(z) = p(z) from 3 (q is a sorting of p)
  3. [x . p](z) = p(z) because z!=x
  4. r(z) = [x . p](z)
7.2.2.3. continuation of inductive case
  1. r(z) = [x . p](z) from 14 and 18.
  2. r is a sorting of p from 9 and 19 using the definition of sorting.

The algorithm for insertion sort a simple transliteration of the isort relation: Here is the Scheme version of insertion sort.

8. Merge Sort

Logic is divided into two parts - one for merge and other for mergesort 


----------------------------- MSN
mergesort [] = []

------------------------------------------------ MSS
mergesort [x] = [x]

([x] denotes a singleton array)


mergesort(xs) = a1 , mergesort(ys) = a2
-------------------------------------------------------------- MSI
mergesort (xs : ys) = merge (a1,a2)


------------------ MN1
merge([],p) = p

------------------ MN2
merge(p,[]) = p

merge(p, [y . q]) = r, x <=y
-----------------------------------  MI1
merge([x . p], [y . q]) = [x . r]

merge([x . p], q) = r, y < x
----------------------------------- MI2
merge([x . p], [y . q]) = [y . r]

Lemma:
  sorted(p) AND sorted(q) => sorted(merge([x . p], [y . q])) for all x,y.

8.1. Proof

Based on induction on the sum of the lengths of p and q.

9. Quicksort


-------------------------  nil
partitioned(x, [], [])

partitioned(x,l,r)   x <=y
--------------------------   R
partitioned(x, l, [y . r])

partitioned(x,l,r)   y < x
--------------------------- L
partitioned(x, [y . l], r)



-----------------------    nil
split([],x) = ([], [])


split(p, x) = (l, r)     x < y
--------------------------------   R
